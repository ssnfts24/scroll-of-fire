01 · Purpose

Not only a calendar—a living observatory

The 13 Moons Remnant Living Time Observatory is a living time, witness, pattern-recognition, research, and reality-observation system. The fixed calendar supplies a stable frame. The sky, Earth, environment, community, invention, and witness supply the changing field.

Its purpose is to reveal how cycles align, separate, recur, transform, and become observable across time without forcing symbolic interpretation into scientific fact.

The fixed pattern makes movement measurable. The moving world makes the pattern alive. The witness preserves what occurred.

03 · Hybrid A

Stable Pattern Time, moving astronomical time

Pattern Calendar

  • 13 moons
  • 28 days per moon
  • 4 weeks per moon
  • 7 days per week
  • 364 structured days
  • Day Out of Time
  • Deep Time Day during leap years

Confirmed Pattern anchor

April 17, 2026 = Moon 1 · Day 1 · Day 1/364.

This makes July 17, 2026 equal to Moon 4 · Day 8 · Day 92/364, matching the current live calendar.

Leap handling

The arithmetic leap structure remains predictable. Intercalary days exist outside the 13 moons, 52 weeks, and ordinary weekday cycle. Astronomical events do not directly change the fixed Pattern Calendar. Their relationship to it is measured separately.

04 · Equinox Passage

The changing interval before the Pattern New Year

The Equinox Passage is the variable interval between the astronomical March Equinox Gate and Moon 1 Day 1 of the fixed Pattern Calendar.

Passage begins

The exact astronomical March Equinox Gate.

Passage ends

The beginning of Moon 1 Day 1.

Passage measures

Days, hours, local boundaries, lunar conditions, light, and response.

Passage purpose

Reveal how Earth Time and fixed Pattern Time move in relationship.

The Earth crosses balance at the Equinox Gate. The Pattern Year completes its final movement through the Equinox Passage. Moon 1 Day 1 begins the new structured year.

09 · Research Integrity

Truth before defense of the system

  • Timestamp predictions before outcomes.
  • Define hypotheses and variables.
  • Record failed predictions.
  • Preserve negative results.
  • Use comparison groups where possible.
  • Separate correlation from causation.
  • Publish methods and limitations.
  • Preserve raw observations.
  • Allow replication.
  • Protect privacy and obtain consent.
  • Avoid diagnostic or medical claims.
  • Label uncertainty clearly.
A failed prediction is also a witness.

Integration rules
