Part III · Living Systems

Living Technology

Technology begins wherever material, knowledge, intention, and skilled action become a working system.

Living Technology is the Codex framework for tools and artifacts that remain connected to their materials, maker, operator, environment, use, memory, maintenance, and ethical consequence. A device does not need a battery to contain engineering, information, or accumulated craft.

LT

Living does not mean biologically alive. It means the technology remains relational, revisable, maintainable, and connected to the conditions of its use.

Orientation

Craft is technology when knowledge becomes repeatable action.

Material intelligence

A maker learns how copper bends, stone fractures, wood moves, fiber tensions, leather wears, adhesives cure, and tools transfer force.

Embodied procedure

Technique is stored in sequence, timing, pressure, angle, grip, correction, and judgment—not only in written instructions.

Relational use

The operator, environment, maintenance, purpose, and witness all affect how a tool or artifact functions in practice.

Visible limits

Symbolic meaning, personal experience, material fact, and experimentally supported performance should remain clearly distinguished.

Technology Spectrum

From simple tool to living system.

1 Material

A resource with properties, history, limits, and potential use.

2 Tool

Material shaped to extend human force, accuracy, reach, memory, or perception.

3 Technique

A repeatable sequence of actions that allows the tool to function.

4 Artifact

A made object carrying construction, purpose, symbolism, record, and stewardship.

5 Instrument

An artifact used to produce, detect, organize, or compare an observable effect.

6 System

Multiple components, operators, environments, procedures, and feedback paths working together.

7 Living Technology

A system that remains documented, maintainable, relational, ethically bounded, and open to correction.

Claim Layers

Material fact and symbolic meaning can coexist without becoming confused.

Layer 1

Material fact

What the object is made from, how it is constructed, its dimensions, mass, visible condition, and established material properties.

Layer 2

Designed function

What the builder intended the object to do and what procedures are required for use.

Layer 3

Observed behavior

What was measured or directly witnessed under stated conditions.

Layer 4

Interpretation

The proposed explanation for the observed behavior, including uncertainty and alternatives.

Layer 5

Symbolic meaning

The name, mythology, spiritual association, artistic language, memory, and personal significance.

Layer 6

Performance claim

A statement that the object reliably causes a defined effect. This layer requires the strongest method and evidence.

Build Protocol

From source material to registered artifact.

Document the object while it is becoming.

A final photograph cannot preserve the decisions, failures, substitutions, repairs, and source conditions that created the artifact.

1 · Declare purpose

State what is being built, for whom, for what use, and with what claim boundary.

2 · Record source

List materials, origin, condition, dimensions, supplier, reclaimed status, and substitutions.

3 · Draw the design

Preserve sketches, geometry, intended assembly, interfaces, and unresolved questions.

4 · Mark the version

Assign a build number before construction so failed forms are not erased from history.

5 · Build in stages

Photograph and record each major layer before it becomes hidden by the next one.

6 · Inspect materially

Check sharp edges, loose parts, exposed conductors, heat, adhesives, structural weakness, and wear points.

7 · Establish a baseline

Record the object and environment before any active field or operator test.

8 · Run controlled witness

Change one major variable at a time and preserve direct observation separately from interpretation.

9 · Review ethically

Confirm consent, safe use, claim accuracy, public language, and appropriate halt conditions.

10 · Register the Remnant

Publish the strongest verified build history, declared symbolism, status, limits, and next revision.

Field Testing

A meaningful experience is not automatically a confirmed mechanism.

Baseline

Record the instrument, room, site, operator state, nearby devices, and initial measurements before activation.

Single-variable change

Alter one main feature—orientation, audio, distance, core, magnet position, or operator procedure—when practical.

Controls

Compare against inactive, absent, reversed, shielded, or visually similar conditions where appropriate.

Replication

Repeat the test across time, location, operator, instrument, or observer before strengthening the claim.

Alternative causes

Consider ordinary material, magnetic, electrical, acoustic, environmental, procedural, and expectation effects.

Claim ladder

Keep “felt,” “observed,” “measured,” “associated,” “repeatable,” and “caused” as different levels.

Living Operators Ethical Law

Maintenance

A living system must survive contact with time.

1 Inspect

Check wear, corrosion, looseness, fracture, insulation, adhesive, finish, and deformation.

2 Clean

Use material-appropriate methods and record anything that changes appearance or performance.

3 Repair

Preserve the original structure where possible and document replacement parts.

4 Retest

After repair, repeat the baseline and verify that the system remains safe and usable.

5 Update

Change the registry version, condition, status, photographs, and claim language.

6 Retire

When safe function cannot be preserved, archive the artifact rather than hiding the failure.

Ethical Technology

Power without responsibility is not living technology.

Safe construction

Address sharp materials, magnets, heat, electricity, fragile stone, small parts, adhesives, and structural failure at the design stage.

Transparent claims

Separate material properties, intended function, personal witness, symbolic meaning, and demonstrated performance.

Informed transfer

A keeper should receive use instructions, limitations, maintenance needs, condition, and known risks.

Consent in testing

Do not involve another person in sound, field, suggestion, or behavioral experiments without clear awareness and permission.

Repair responsibility

A maker should not hide a known defect, failed test, or misleading registry entry.

Stewardship

Materials, land, knowledge, records, and artifacts should be handled as relationships carrying consequence—not disposable inputs.
