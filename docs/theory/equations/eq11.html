Canon 11 · Projection Frame

Equation 11

Every view reveals something and leaves something outside the frame.

Equation 11 defines the Projection Frame as the bounded transformation through which a larger field becomes a visible, measurable, narratable, or actionable representation. The frame determines what is selected, emphasized, compressed, omitted, and rendered.

Equation 11

The Projection Frame

$$ \boxed{ F = \mathcal{P}_{\Omega\rightarrow F} \left[ \Psi_{\Omega} \right] } $$

The frame \(F\) is the result of projecting the larger field \( \Psi_{\Omega} \) through a declared projection operator \( \mathcal{P}_{\Omega\rightarrow F} \).

Expanded frame relation

$$ F = \mathcal{E}_{9} \left[ \mathcal{S} \circ \mathcal{G} \circ \mathcal{T} \circ \mathcal{R} \left( \Psi_{\Omega} \right) \right] $$

The projection may include Selection \( \mathcal{S} \), Gating \( \mathcal{G} \), Transformation \( \mathcal{T} \), and Rendering \( \mathcal{R} \), all contained by Ethical Law \( \mathcal{E}_{9} \).

Plain Language

What Equation 11 means.

No map shows every detail of the territory. No photograph contains the entire event. No chart displays every variable. No story preserves every voice. A projection frame is useful because it reduces complexity, but the reduction must remain visible enough that readers do not mistake the frame for the whole.

Selection

The frame determines which people, variables, times, sources, objects, or signals enter.

Emphasis

Scale, position, repetition, color, ordering, language, and weight determine what appears most important.

Compression

A complex field may be reduced into a summary, graph, story, category, score, map, image, or model.

Omission

Everything outside the frame does not disappear from reality merely because it is not rendered.

Frame Layers

Seven decisions inside every projection.

1 Boundary

What field, event, time range, population, object, or system is included?

2 Position

From whose location, instrument, role, scale, or perspective is the field viewed?

3 Selection

Which variables, records, voices, images, or events enter the frame?

4 Weight

Which selected elements receive more prominence, confidence, scale, or authority?

5 Transformation

How are units translated, grouped, normalized, summarized, or compressed?

6 Rendering

How is the result presented: graph, story, image, map, table, interface, equation, or ritual?

7 Revision

How can the frame be challenged, widened, corrected, or replaced?

Frame Types

Common ways a field becomes visible.

Spatial frame

A map, room, boundary, route, artifact layout, site plan, or geographic view.

Temporal frame

A moment, day, week, moon, season, historical period, timeline, or memory window.

Numerical frame

A graph, average, distribution, ratio, index, score, table, or normalized scale.

Narrative frame

A story structure determining beginning, conflict, agency, motive, sequence, and conclusion.

Visual frame

A crop, angle, lens, contrast, background, juxtaposition, caption, or image sequence.

Operational frame

A dashboard, interface, checklist, protocol, workflow, or decision surface.

Projection Loss

Every frame leaves information behind.

$$ \mathcal{L}_{F} = \Psi_{\Omega} - \mathcal{P}^{-1}_{F\rightarrow\Omega} \left[ F \right] $$

Projection loss \( \mathcal{L}_{F} \) represents what cannot be fully recovered from the rendered frame alone. The inverse term is conceptual: many frames cannot reconstruct the entire source field.

Lost detail

A summary may remove exact timing, individual variation, sequence, or source context.

Lost voice

A single narrative may exclude participants whose account does not fit the chosen structure.

Lost uncertainty

A chart or category may make fuzzy boundaries appear exact.

Lost scale

A close-up may hide the larger environment; a wide view may erase small but important detail.

Multi-Frame Method

One field can support several valid projections.

$$ \mathcal{F}_{\Omega} = \left\{ F_1,F_2,\ldots,F_m \right\} $$

A multi-frame set compares several projections of the same source field. Agreement may strengthen a conclusion. Difference may reveal scale, position, omission, or transformation effects that one frame hides.

Material frame

What is physically present, measured, assembled, damaged, or preserved?

Witness frame

What did each observer directly experience under known conditions?

Historical frame

How did the system, object, or event develop over time?

Symbolic frame

What meanings, names, narratives, correspondences, or rituals are attached?

Technical frame

What mechanisms, inputs, outputs, dependencies, and measurable limits are present?

Ethical frame

Who is affected, who consents, who bears risk, and who can stop or correct the process?

Frame Protocol

From source field to honest representation.

1 · Name the source field

Define the event, archive, system, object, population, or time range being projected.

2 · Name the purpose

State what the frame is meant to explain, compare, reveal, guide, persuade, or decide.

3 · Declare the observer position

Record role, instrument, location, scale, access, interest, and limitation.

4 · Declare selection rules

State what enters, what remains outside, and why.

5 · Declare transformations

Document normalization, translation, aggregation, editing, coding, weighting, or compression.

6 · Declare the rendering

Choose chart, story, map, image, table, interface, equation, or another form suited to the purpose.

7 · Identify projection loss

List detail, voice, uncertainty, context, scale, or sequence that the frame cannot preserve.

8 · Compare alternate frames

Change scale, source, time range, viewpoint, category, or rendering to test what changes.

9 · Apply Ethical Law

Review privacy, dignity, consent, proportion, transparency, accountability, and correction.

10 · Preserve revision history

Record how the frame changed and which earlier versions remain in the archive.

Worked Example 1

A photograph of an artifact.

Source field

The artifact, room, light, background, camera, operator, and surrounding objects.

Selection

The crop includes the artifact and excludes most of the work area.

Transformation

Exposure, contrast, focus, perspective, color balance, and background replacement alter presentation.

Rendering

A promotional image emphasizes the artifact’s form and symbolic identity.

Projection loss

Scale, weight, hidden construction, back side, flaws, and full environmental context may be absent.

Ethical correction

Do not edit the image so heavily that material, condition, color, or finish becomes misleading.

Worked Example 2

A timeline built from messages and reports.

Source field

Messages, reports, dates, witnesses, court orders, police records, schedules, and later corrections.

Selection risk

Choosing only one side’s strongest messages can create a distorted frame.

Transformation

Events are summarized, ordered, categorized, and connected to claims.

Projection loss

Tone, missing messages, unknown context, private circumstances, and unrecorded events may remain outside.

Better frame

Use exact dates, quote sparingly, preserve full exhibits, separate direct evidence from interpretation, and attach contradictions.

Ethical boundary

Protect children and unnecessary private information while preserving relevant evidence and correction.

Worked Example 3

A frequency console dashboard.

Source field

Multiple carriers, gains, gates, timing, routing, visualizations, presets, witness logs, and device state.

Operational frame

The dashboard exposes only the controls needed for the current task.

Weighting

Master volume, mute, stop, current preset, and active carriers receive highest visibility.

Projection loss

Hidden implementation detail, browser audio behavior, device differences, and historical settings may not appear on the main screen.

Alternate frame

An observatory view may expose waveform, timeline, carrier values, and logs for deeper analysis.

Ethical correction

Stop and mute controls must remain obvious, working, and accessible.

Worked Example 4

A recurring number pattern.

Source field

All times, receipts, addresses, counts, dates, and numbers encountered during the chosen period.

Selected frame

Only occurrences matching a meaningful number are collected.

Projection effect

The selected matches become highly visible while nonmatching numbers disappear from the frame.

Symbolic frame

The pattern may still serve as a personal mirror, prompt, or memory marker.

Analytical frame

A complete log of matches and nonmatches is needed before claiming unusual frequency.

Proportionate conclusion

The symbolic meaning and statistical claim remain separate projections of the same witness field.

Scope

What Equation 11 does and does not do.

It does

Make selection, viewpoint, transformation, rendering, omission, and revision visible.

It does not

Claim that every viewpoint is equally accurate or that a frame can replace the underlying field.

It supports clarity

Good framing can reduce complexity enough to make action, comparison, teaching, or testing possible.

It requires humility

Every frame should disclose enough of its boundary that readers can recognize what remains outside.

Failure Modes

How a frame becomes distortion.

Frame as total reality

A chart, story, image, category, or model is treated as the complete field.

Hidden selection

The audience is not told which records, voices, dates, trials, or variables were excluded.

Manipulative scale

Axes, crop, contrast, category boundaries, or time range exaggerate or conceal change.

Perspective erasure

One observer’s position becomes universal while others disappear.

Compression without warning

Summaries remove uncertainty, disagreement, sequence, or exception without disclosure.

Uncorrectable frame

The representation cannot be challenged, widened, revised, or compared against source material.

Ethical Boundary

Projection cannot become concealment.

$$ F_{\mathrm{allowed}} = \mathcal{E}_{9} \left[ \mathcal{P}_{\Omega\rightarrow F} \left( \Psi_{\Omega} \right) \right] $$

A useful frame may simplify, but it must not knowingly conceal decisive evidence, erase affected people, misrepresent scale, expose unnecessary private information, or prevent correction.

Privacy

Do not include personal details merely because they exist inside the source field.

Truth

Disclose significant selection, transformation, uncertainty, editing, and limitation.

Dignity

Do not reduce people to labels, data points, villains, symbols, or background material.

Correction

Keep the source record and revision path available when the frame influences real decisions.
