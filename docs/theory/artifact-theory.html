Artifact Theory

An object becomes an artifact when its making, meaning, memory, and stewardship remain visible.

Artifact Theory explains how a made object enters the Codex as more than inventory. A registered artifact carries materials, labor, design, provenance, symbolic meaning, witness, condition, status, transfer, repair, and the responsibility of whoever keeps it.

Open the Artifact Form Study the Registry Layers Use the Protocol Open Artifact Registry
AR

The registry does not create the object’s history. It makes that history visible enough to preserve, correct, transfer, and continue.

Orientation

Artifact is a relationship, not merely a category.

Made

The artifact contains material decisions, tool use, time, failure, revision, skill, and the physical trace of construction.

Named

A name places the object inside language, memory, class, mythology, and the larger Codex system.

Witnessed

Photographs, build notes, measurements, keeper reports, repairs, field tests, and condition updates form the visible record.

Stewarded

The artifact continues through storage, use, gifting, sale, transfer, maintenance, archive, and eventual retirement.

Status is time-dependent

Available, Reserved, Gifted, Personal Instrument, In Creation, Coming Soon, Archived, Retired, and Not for Sale are not permanent identities. The registry should record when status changes and why.

Registry Layers

Seven layers make an artifact legible.

M

Material

Copper, stone, glass, ferrite, magnets, fiber, leather, wood, adhesive, reclaimed source, dimensions, condition, and material limits.

D

Design

Geometry, weave, winding, structure, fastening, stone placement, interfaces, version, and intended function.

N

Name

Title, translation, code, class, subtitle, story, symbolic language, and position inside the Codex.

P

Provenance

Maker, date, location, source materials, ownership, gifting, sale, transfer, and chain of stewardship.

W

Witness

Build photographs, field notes, measurements, direct observation, keeper reports, condition checks, and documented revision.

Mτ

Memory

The accumulated story of making, use, travel, repair, transfer, interpretation, and later remembrance.

St

Status

Current availability, ownership, release stage, condition, registry visibility, and permitted transfer.

Classification

Artifact classes describe relationship and use.

Personal Instrument

Built for the maker or a designated operator. Usually retained, tested, and developed as part of the active Living Technology system.

Wearable Artifact

A ring, band, pendant, wrist node, or other piece designed to be carried on the body.

Gifted Artifact

Transferred without sale as a deliberate relationship between maker, object, and keeper.

Registry Release

An artifact made available for purchase or inquiry with visible materials, status, price, and transfer terms.

Archive Piece

Retained as a completed historical object, reference build, earlier version, or non-released record.

Unreleased / In Creation

A work whose final structure, name, status, price, or claim language has not yet been completed.

Naming

A name should deepen the object without hiding what it is.

1 Registry code

A stable identifier such as T7-000, HL-001, TN-001, or TS-001.

2 Primary name

The recognized title used across the registry, images, posts, and documentation.

3 Translation or subtitle

A second language, descriptive phrase, or mythic title that adds context.

4 Class

The practical relationship: Personal Instrument, Gifted Artifact, Wearable Artifact, Archive Piece, or Registry Release.

5 Material description

A direct account of what the object is physically made from.

6 Mythic description

The symbolic story, archetype, memory, or meaning carried by the name.

7 Claim boundary

A clear distinction between symbolism, maker intention, keeper witness, and demonstrated performance.

Provenance

The chain of becoming belongs to the artifact.

Material origin

Reclaimed, purchased, found, gifted, inherited, locally gathered, or salvaged materials should be described accurately.

Maker record

Who built the artifact, when, where, under what project, and through which version or revision.

Transfer record

Gift, sale, reservation, inheritance, return, or keeper change should be dated when the parties choose to preserve that history.

Condition record

Damage, repair, patina, wear, missing parts, replacement materials, and restoration belong in the continuing provenance.

Provenance should not expose private personal information unnecessarily. The registry can preserve continuity while limiting names, addresses, transaction details, or other identifying data.

Claim Layers

What an artifact means is not always what it proves.

Material

Verified composition

The metals, stones, glass, fibers, magnets, cores, fasteners, coatings, and visible construction.

Design

Declared function

What the maker intended the structure, geometry, arrangement, or wearable form to do.

Witness

Observed experience

What a maker or keeper directly noticed, measured, felt, or recorded under stated conditions.

Interpretation

Proposed meaning

The theory connecting the artifact to a field, pattern, memory, carrier, place, or operator experience.

Symbolism

Mythic and personal language

The story, title, archetype, spiritual meaning, cultural reference, or private relationship carried by the artifact.

Performance

Repeatable effect

A claim that the artifact reliably causes a defined outcome. This requires the strongest evidence and the narrowest wording.

Registration Protocol

From finished object to living record.

Register what exists—not what the object may someday become.

Future plans, symbolic possibilities, and testable hypotheses can be included, but the current build, status, condition, and claim level should remain clear.

1 · Assign the code

Create a unique identifier that will remain stable through later revisions and status changes.

2 · Confirm the name

Record the primary title, translation, subtitle, and pronunciation when relevant.

3 · Set the class

Choose the artifact class based on actual relationship and intended use.

4 · List materials

Describe the visible and hidden materials, including reclaimed or substituted components.

5 · Record dimensions

Measure size, mass, fit, and other practical specifications when useful.

6 · Preserve build witness

Attach photographs, notes, sketches, dates, failed versions, and important construction changes.

7 · Write the two descriptions

Separate the material description from the mythic or symbolic artifact scroll.

8 · Declare status

Available, Reserved, Gifted, Personal, In Creation, Coming Soon, Archived, Retired, or Not for Sale.

9 · Set the claim boundary

State what is verified, intended, witnessed, theoretical, symbolic, or unresolved.

10 · Publish and version

Release the registry entry with a date, current images, and a visible path for later updates.

Lifecycle

An artifact moves through states.

01 Concept

The idea, sketch, material search, and initial symbolic frame.

02 In Creation

Construction has begun, but the form and registry entry remain open.

03 Completed

The physical build is stable enough for inspection, photography, and classification.

04 Registered

The artifact receives its code, name, materials, status, and public record.

05 Active

The artifact is worn, used, tested, displayed, gifted, offered, or held as a personal instrument.

06 Maintained

Cleaning, repair, resizing, rewinding, replacement, or condition updates preserve continuity.

07 Transferred

Stewardship changes through gift, sale, inheritance, return, or reassignment.

08 Archived or Retired

The artifact leaves active use but remains part of the historical registry.

Stewardship

Ownership does not erase relationship.

Care

Store, clean, wear, transport, and display the artifact according to its actual materials and construction.

Continuity

Preserve the code, name, maker, core materials, and known history when transferring or modifying the object.

Correction

Notify the registry when an important material, condition, status, or performance description is found to be wrong.

Respect

Symbolic meaning can be changed by a keeper, but the original maker’s declared meaning should not be silently rewritten as though it never existed.

Repair

Record visible repairs and replacement materials when they alter the object’s form, function, value, or history.

Release

A steward may gift, sell, return, archive, or retire an artifact according to the terms and responsibilities attached to it.

Ethical Registry

Mythic presentation must not become deceptive representation.

Accurate materials

Do not misidentify stone, metal, glass, coating, magnet type, reclaimed source, or construction.

Accurate status

Available, Reserved, Gifted, Personal, and Sold should match the object’s actual status.

Accurate images

Promotional backgrounds may be stylized, but the artifact itself should remain recognizable and not materially altered without disclosure.

Accurate claims

Separate symbolism, intention, personal witness, and testable performance.

Transparent price

When offered, the price, availability method, shipping or transfer terms, and major condition issues should be clear.

Privacy

A gifted or transferred artifact does not require public disclosure of the keeper’s identity.
