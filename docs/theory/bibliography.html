Bibliography

A theory becomes clearer when its sources, comparisons, inheritances, and original contributions remain visible.

This page organizes the source architecture of the Codex of Reality. It separates internal project documents, primary witness, scientific and technical references, historical comparisons, philosophical influences, traditional material, and unresolved attribution.

Source Classes Internal Codex External Reading Source Template
REF

Influence is not identity. Comparison is not equivalence. Citation is not automatic agreement. Every source must be placed inside a visible role.

Principles

How sources enter the Codex.

1 Name the source

Record author, title, date, publisher or archive, and a stable location when available.

2 Name the role

State whether the source supplies fact, method, historical context, comparison, inspiration, symbolism, or disagreement.

3 Separate origin from adaptation

Show what the external source says and what the Codex changes, extends, or rejects.

4 Prefer primary material

Use original papers, books, standards, records, measurements, and archived documents when practical.

5 Preserve uncertainty

When attribution, date, authorship, translation, or interpretation is unresolved, mark it unresolved.

6 Correct publicly

When a citation or comparison is wrong, update both the bibliography and the pages that depend on it.

Source Classes

Eight source labels.

COD

Codex-origin

Material authored inside the Scroll of Fire / Codex of Reality project, including equations, theory chapters, artifact scrolls, and system documentation.

PRI

Primary source

Original research, measurement, historical document, technical standard, firsthand record, or work by the person whose ideas are being discussed.

SEC

Secondary source

Analysis, interpretation, history, review, commentary, or synthesis built from primary material.

CMP

Comparator

An external model used to compare structure, behavior, or consequence without being absorbed into the Codex as its origin.

TRD

Traditional source

Religious, mythic, linguistic, oral, artistic, or inherited material whose historical layers require careful attribution.

WIT

Witness source

A dated firsthand observation, field note, message, photograph, measurement, or keeper report.

ARC

Archive source

A preserved repository, registry, file collection, public record, library, museum, or versioned project archive.

UNR

Unresolved source

A reference whose exact origin, authorship, wording, date, or relevance has not yet been confirmed.

External Reading

Technical, philosophical, and historical context.

The works below are not presented as proof of the Codex. They provide vocabulary, methods, comparisons, historical context, or productive disagreement. Each equation page should identify the exact role a source plays.

Systems and Cybernetics

Feedback, organization, learning, and constraint.

PRI / CMP

Norbert Wiener — Cybernetics

A foundational reference for feedback, control, communication, and circular causation in animals and machines.

PRI / CMP

W. Ross Ashby — An Introduction to Cybernetics

Useful for variety, regulation, state transitions, stability, and the design of adaptive systems.

SEC / CMP

Donella H. Meadows — Thinking in Systems

A practical systems reference for stocks, flows, feedback loops, delays, leverage points, and unintended consequence.

SEC / CMP

Peter M. Senge — The Fifth Discipline

Relevant to learning systems, mental models, shared vision, feedback, and organizational adaptation.

SEC / CMP

Gregory Bateson — Steps to an Ecology of Mind

A broad comparison point for pattern, relationship, communication, learning, context, and ecological thinking.

Information and Language

Signal, meaning, medium, and interpretation.

PRI

Claude E. Shannon — “A Mathematical Theory of Communication”

A primary reference for mathematical information, signal transmission, uncertainty, channel capacity, and noise. Semantic meaning remains outside Shannon’s narrow technical measure.

SEC / CMP

Marshall McLuhan — Understanding Media

Relevant to the relationship among medium, perception, social organization, and the effects of communication technologies.

SEC / CMP

George Lakoff and Mark Johnson — Metaphors We Live By

Useful for examining how metaphor structures thought, framing, abstraction, and everyday interpretation.

SEC / CMP

Ferdinand de Saussure — Course in General Linguistics

A historical reference for sign, signifier, signified, difference, and structural approaches to language.

Witness and Perception

Situated observation, embodiment, and experience.

PRI / CMP

James J. Gibson — The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception

A major comparison point for perception as active relation between organism and environment rather than passive internal picture-making.

SEC / CMP

Francisco J. Varela, Evan Thompson, and Eleanor Rosch — The Embodied Mind

Relevant to embodiment, cognition, lived experience, and the relation between first-person and scientific inquiry.

SEC / CMP

Daniel Kahneman — Thinking, Fast and Slow

Useful for examining attention, judgment, heuristics, bias, confidence, memory, and decision-making.

SEC / CMP

Thomas S. Kuhn — The Structure of Scientific Revolutions

A historical and philosophical comparison for paradigms, normal science, anomaly, crisis, and conceptual change.

Memory and Archive

Retention, reconstruction, record, and cultural continuity.

SEC / CMP

Frederic C. Bartlett — Remembering

A classic reference for reconstructive memory, schema, retelling, and the transformation of remembered material.

SEC / CMP

Maurice Halbwachs — On Collective Memory

Relevant to how groups, institutions, and social frameworks shape the preservation and interpretation of memory.

SEC / CMP

Mary Douglas — How Institutions Think

Useful for understanding classification, institutional memory, shared categories, and durable social patterns.

SEC / CMP

Jacques Derrida — Archive Fever

A philosophical comparison for archive, authority, preservation, exclusion, and the power involved in deciding what remains.

Sound and Acoustics

Frequency, hearing, waveform, resonance, and musical perception.

PRI / Historical

Hermann von Helmholtz — On the Sensations of Tone

A foundational historical work connecting acoustics, hearing, resonance, timbre, consonance, and musical perception.

PRI / Historical

Joseph Fourier — The Analytical Theory of Heat

Historically important to the analysis of complex functions through sinusoidal components, later central to signal and spectral analysis.

SEC / Technical

Arthur H. Benade — Fundamentals of Musical Acoustics

A practical technical reference for sound production, instruments, resonance, waves, rooms, and musical acoustics.

SEC / Technical

Brian C. J. Moore — An Introduction to the Psychology of Hearing

Relevant to pitch, loudness, masking, frequency resolution, binaural hearing, and auditory perception.

Craft and Material Culture

Making, skill, tools, objects, and embodied knowledge.

SEC / CMP

Richard Sennett — The Craftsman

A major comparison for skill, repetition, material engagement, quality, workshop knowledge, and the ethics of making.

SEC / CMP

David Pye — The Nature and Art of Workmanship

Useful for distinctions among workmanship, risk, certainty, hand process, industrial process, and the quality of made objects.

SEC / CMP

Tim Ingold — Making

Relevant to materials, movement, form, correspondence, environment, embodied practice, and the process of becoming through making.

SEC / CMP

Glenn Adamson — Thinking Through Craft

A contemporary comparison for craft theory, material practice, design, art, labor, and cultural classification.

SEC / CMP

Arjun Appadurai, editor — The Social Life of Things

Useful for following objects through exchange, value, classification, status, ownership, and changing social meaning.

Ethics, Governance, and Stewardship

Responsibility, commons, care, and consequence.

PRI / CMP

Elinor Ostrom — Governing the Commons

A major reference for shared-resource governance, institutional diversity, accountability, monitoring, boundaries, and locally developed rules.

SEC / CMP

Hans Jonas — The Imperative of Responsibility

Relevant to technology, future consequence, restraint, responsibility, and obligations created by expanded human power.

SEC / CMP

Joan C. Tronto — Moral Boundaries

Useful for care as practice, responsibility, attentiveness, competence, responsiveness, and political organization.

SEC / CMP

Langdon Winner — The Whale and the Reactor

A comparison point for the politics of artifacts, technological systems, power, design, and social consequence.

Time, Calendar, and Astronomy

E. G. Richards — Mapping Time: The Calendar and Its History

A broad reference for calendar construction, reform, chronology, astronomical cycles, and civil timekeeping.

SEC / Historical

Traditional and Symbolic Sources

Use inherited material without flattening its history.

Hebrew language and script

Distinguish Biblical Hebrew, later Hebrew forms, modern usage, Paleo-Hebrew reconstruction, transliteration, translation, and personal symbolic adaptation.

Biblical and related texts

Identify the specific book, passage, language, translation, textual tradition, and interpretive layer being used.

Mythic names

An artifact name may draw from a traditional language or story, but the registry should not imply historical antiquity for a newly created object.

Sacred geometry

Separate measurable geometry from historical symbolism, modern esoteric interpretation, and claims of physical effect.

Number symbolism

Mark numerological meaning as symbolic unless a separate mathematical or empirical relation is explicitly demonstrated.

Oral and family history

Preserve who told the account, when it was recorded, what documents support it, and which details remain uncertain.

Citation Style

A practical internal format.

$$ [\text{CLASS}:\text{AUTHOR}-\text{YEAR}:\text{LOCATOR}] $$

The Codex may use a compact internal label in notes, followed by a full bibliographic entry on this page or inside the relevant equation chapter.

Book

Author. Title. Edition when relevant. Publisher, year.

Article or paper

Author. “Title.” Journal or publication, volume and issue when relevant, year, pages, DOI or stable archive.

Website or living document

Author or organization. Page title. Site title. Publication or revision date, archived version or access date when content may change.

Witness record

Witness name or protected identifier. Record type. Date, time, location, file or ledger ID, and custody note.

Artifact

Maker. Artifact code and name. Materials, date, class, registry version, and current status.

Repository file

Project. File path. Commit or revision identifier, date, and relevant line, section, or version.

Source Record

Template for adding a new reference.

1 · Source class

COD, PRI, SEC, CMP, TRD, WIT, ARC, or UNR.

2 · Full attribution

Author, title, edition, publication, date, stable identifier, and locator.

3 · Source role

Fact, method, comparison, historical context, symbolic influence, disagreement, or archive.

4 · Relevant passage or concept

Summarize the part actually used without copying more than necessary.

5 · Codex relation

Identify the equation, term, chapter, artifact, calendar rule, or system feature connected to the source.

6 · Difference

State where the Codex agrees, adapts, extends, reframes, or rejects the external model.

7 · Confidence and limitations

Note translation issues, missing pages, disputed authorship, secondary dependence, or unresolved interpretation.

8 · Revision date

Record when the source was added, corrected, replaced, or reclassified.

Revision Standard

The bibliography is a living record.

New sources may strengthen, narrow, contradict, or replace earlier comparisons. When that happens, the bibliography, Canon page, glossary, and affected system chapter should be revised together. A source should never remain listed merely because it supports a preferred conclusion.
